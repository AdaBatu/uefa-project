# In possession of the ball

In the following we will analyze the attacking behaviour of Napoli.

<details>
<summary>Passes</summary>

- The code can be found here for generating the plots.
[Passes](https://gitlab.ethz.ch/socceranalytics/uefa-cl-2025-2026/-/wikis/Passes)


![pass_network](uploads/4def8a44aa880e128b9fe56e856091f2/pass_network.jpeg)

As the data from Manchester City match suggests, Napoli try to play progressively with direct passes in build-up. For this game Sam Beukema and Stanislav Lobotka  acted as the connection between defence and attack. The progression patterns suggests that the build-up is heavier on the left side. This will become more clear in the pass volume heatmap. Regarding the attack shape, the forward players are positioned relatively narrow around the central corridor. The network is not very wing-isolation heavy and long balls are not preferred. Instead the team attempts to access central zones with direct passes.

![napoli_pass_volume_heatmap](uploads/275be3a9baf5a0c549934ff664733dcc/napoli_pass_volume_heatmap.png)

Highest activity is around central midfield and half-spaces. The interesting point is that, Napoli did not manage to have much activity with the ball in and around the opponent penalty box. They usually lose control of the ball after the final third. This data also matches the pass network data for Manchester City game as they have more volume on the left half-space.

![napoli_passing_volume_efficiency](uploads/1481bfd40651bcd9a586f8d2d80ae844/napoli_passing_volume_efficiency.png)

This plot now strengthens our conclusions from before. The main takeaway is that Napoli are extremely active in circulation and decent at moving the ball forward. The overall passing volume indicates strong control phases, high-possession football and patient build-up. The progressive passes and final third access is actually pretty impressive. They can advance territory and break midfield lines especially with their high-quality midfielders including Kevin De Bruyne (although he only could play 3 games because of his injury), Scott McTominay and Eljif Elmas). The issue is not progression, but rather the final action. This will be elaborated more in the coming section.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Overall attack</summary>

![napoly_radar_attack](uploads/39adf6c90ffe1a1e7adffcf419f9c30e/napoly_radar_attack.png)

This radar chart is the most informative attack data summarizing every aspect covered so far. Progressive carries, passes and the access to the final third are way better than the league average. Also, they have managed to find passes into the penalty area. The ball progression quality and entering advanced zones is high. The problem is that these actions don't contribute much to the score, as the final actions are poor. Indeed, they are one of the worst teams in the metric OVB (on-ball-value), which estimates how much the actions increase scoring probability. regarding other metrics about goal scoring, Napoli falls behind too.  They struggle creating xG from open play despite their high-progression and box-entries.

Common reason to this struggle with final execution and chance creation mechanics could be poor spacing inside the box, weaker striker movement and predictable final passes. An elite striker could be able to either score goals from low xG shots or create more chances with dummy runs or better positioning.

![napoli_attack_shot_map_rotated_half](uploads/1576adcde8fdab7a9e987fb97a3682e3/napoli_attack_shot_map_rotated_half.png)

The shot map reinforces the idea that Napoli are a possession-oriented side that can consistently move the ball into dangerous attacking areas, but do not always convert that territorial dominance into truly high-quality chances. Most of their shots come from central positions around the penalty area, which is generally a positive sign because central shots tend to carry higher expected goal values than attempts from wide angles. The clustering of shots around the edge of the six-yard box and the penalty spot suggests that Napoli are capable of progressing through midfield and entering advanced zones with regularity rather than relying on speculative long-range efforts or hopeful crosses.

At the same time, the distribution and size of the circles indicate that many of these attempts are only medium-quality opportunities rather than clear-cut chances. There are relatively few very large xG shots directly in front of goal, which suggests that opponents are often still defensively organized when Napoli take their shots. This fits well with the earlier passing and progression data: Napoli appear technically strong in buildup and final-third access, but slightly less effective at creating the kind of defensive disorganization that leads to easy finishes or tap-ins. Overall, the shot map portrays a team that sustains attacking pressure and generates a solid volume of central shots, yet lacks the final layer of penetration and efficiency seen in Europe’s most clinical attacking sides.

# Transition after losing the ball

- The code can be found here for generating the plots.
[Source Code](https://gitlab.ethz.ch/socceranalytics/uefa-cl-2025-2026/-/wikis/Helper-Python-files)

<details>
<summary>Ball losses</summary>

![ball_losses](uploads/44bd55c51cd812ad460df519c1d86a56/ball_losses.jpeg)

The ball loss map shows a slight bias toward Napoli’s left attacking side, with lost balls increasing as they progress further up the pitch. There’s also a noticeable cluster of losses near the corners, suggesting that crosses or short corner routines are often ending in immediate ball losses. Additionally, a small concentration of losses just near the 5 meter line points to issues in precise goal kicks or retaining possession after goal kicks, where the first or second pass under pressure may be breaking down.

![napoli_loss_to_shot_rate_vs_average](uploads/1271d826fa6e751177519e2d6771b31f/napoli_loss_to_shot_rate_vs_average.png)

The heatmap shows that Napoli’s center backs are not a major source of danger, as they rarely lose the ball in situations that lead to opposition shots. In contrast, the left side is more problematic, with a higher number of ball losses that result in shots. The most significant vulnerability, however, lies in central midfield, where turnovers frequently allow the opposition to progress directly to a shot. This suggests that losses in this area are not sufficiently controlled, making central midfield the key zone of defensive weakness.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Pressure and defensive actions</summary>

![pressure_heatmap](uploads/79534e6b1bf2baf8035acd527ebe226c/pressure_heatmap.jpeg)

As we can see, the pressure is relatively dense in the opponent's half and wide spaces. Napoli tries to step high when possible and press in the opponent's build-up and progression zones. That the activity in the center is not so high can be linked to Napoli's defensive strategy, which is to funnel the play outwards and to force the opponent to build-up through the flanks. They don't open up the play too aggressively and prefer to press near sidelines while keeping the center crowded. The recovery data also supports this as most of the balls won in the opponent's half are near the right touchline.

![interception_heatmap](uploads/560630f8fd4ab11a92867a47e9cb3926/interception_heatmap.jpeg)

You can clearly see from the heatmap that Napoli make the vast majority of their interceptions in their own half. When they do win the ball higher up the pitch, it tends to happen mainly in the wide areas rather than centrally. This pattern suggests that their attacking players apply a good pressing forcing opponents toward the wings, where Napoli can then trap and intercept effectively.

![defensive_actions](uploads/a86675de6e7af30659a89a1aa00c613a/defensive_actions.jpeg)

Pressure dominates the defensive actions by far, which suggests that they pressure intensely after losing the ball. Moreover, especially the ball recovery data is denser in the left side of attacking third, and right side of defensive third. This shows that Napoli tries to force the opponents to the right flank when defending. For the offensive part, also the ball losses are dense in the left attacking third, which means that they preferably attack wide left and therefore lose more balls there and have more chances to regain the possession, supported by pressure data too.

The heat-map further suggests that Napoli's defense is primarily based on active engagement, contesting the ball early in the attacking third and affecting the opponent build-up in their half. After that insense pressure in the very first seconds of ball-loss or a similar action, they then defend deeper.

</details>


<details>
<summary>Ball recovery</summary>

![ball_recovery_heatmap](uploads/3c9fa877bb604cc98c6ef9658c28db4d/ball_recovery_heatmap.jpeg)

Although Napoli presses high in the opponent's half to disturb their build-up, the data suggests that after a while, they wait a little bit deeper, as most of the balls recovered are in the middle third and their own half and only few balls won in the attacking third.

A reason that Napoli wins back the balls deeper is that they force the opponent to play wide and cut their passing channels, forcing weaker next actions, which are then recovered by the second line. So, the data suggests Napoli destabilize the opponent before recovering possession through supporting structure behind the first line.

![recovery_time_after_ball_loss](uploads/38a5f9594bdbe3255aff1bb598848293/recovery_time_after_ball_loss.jpeg)

Napoli’s recovery time distribution highlights an effective counter pressing approach after losing possession. The median recovery time of around 2 to 3 seconds indicates that they typically regain the ball very quickly, while the interquartile range extending only to about 6 to 7 seconds further reinforces how consistently fast these recoveries are. This suggests a well organized and aggressive immediate press.

As expected, the presence of outliers shows that this strategy is not always successful. When the initial counter-press is bypassed, recovery times increase significantly. Linking this to the ball loss map, it is reasonable to assume that losses in the final third are less effectively counter pressed than those closer to Napoli’s own goal, where defensive structure and player density make regaining possession easier.



</details>
